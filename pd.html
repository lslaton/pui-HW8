Non-Black Allyship

Provide tool to help drive non-Black allies to take a more proactive role to fight anti-Black racism.

Context: Persuasive Design Course, CMU
Team: Kraig Fujii, Ally Hopping, Janice Lyu, Divya Mohan, Jenny Xin
Role: Bias Monitor
Timeline: August-December, 2020

Problem

With the growing Black Lives Matter movement in the country, it is crucial that non-Black allies support and aid in the fight against anti-Black racism. While many allies acknowledge racial bias as a problem, they were unsure of what actions to take in both supporting the larger BLM movement, and to address and reduce racial bias in their social circles and communities.

"How might we persuade motivated allies to take a more proactive role in the fight against anti-Black racism?"
Solution

An accountability app that allies use to set monthly goals to complete anti-racist actions that involve educating themselves, engaging members of their social circles in discussions about racism, and involvement with community anti-racist organizations. Users can complete actions to raise money that will be donated to organizations dedicated to fighting racial bias.
My Role

I served as the Bias Monitor in the group, a role specifically designated to check how bias might affect our design process and final intervention. Additionally, I recruited and conducted interviews with racial justice allies, and worked with the team to synthesize our research into concepts and designs based on various persuasive methods.

Research Process

New Metaphor Exercise
Originally, our team had decided to specifically target non-Black college students for our intervention. I facilitated a focus group with four graduate students from colleges in Pittsburgh. The participants selected metaphors that reflected their perspectives on various concepts related to racial bias and discrimination on college campuses. Through this exercise, we found that students are uncertain about what specific or concrete steps to take to engage in anti-racist work. As a result, we see a lack of action and participation from students despite strong support to combat anti-Black racism. We planned to further explore the shared disconnect between attitude and behavior.

User Interviews
In continuing research on allyship, I conducted an interview with a graduate student and member of our target audience. We discussed the larger systemic racism built into settings like schools and hospitals. When it came to their own social sphere, however, my participant echoed the sentiment of other interviewees that addressing racism within their own social sphere was a challenge. They weren't sure of the exact actions to take to address that, but acknowledged that it was something they wanted to improve.

Expert Interviews
As a team, we also conducted interviews with various experts in the field of diversity and racial bias. We discussed the challenges BIPOC students face, student campus resources, pain points with these resources, and effective versus ineffective allyship. One emerging theme that was discussed was the need for allies to step out of their comfort zones and make a conscious effort to engage with and expand their typical social circle.

Project Definition

After conducting the user and expert interviews, our team had initially decided to create an intervention aimed at reducing racial bias on college campuses and motivating non-Black students who view themselves as allies (or recognize racial bias as an ongoing issue) to take concrete action against racism. We began to generate design ideas centered around encouraging more meaningful relationships between racially-diverse groups of students. As many of our ideas could be used in a larger context, however, we decided to expand our target audience to all motivated non-Black allies.

Ideate

After ideating several possible interventions, our team decided on three main concepts to pitch to our class for feedback:

  • An incentivized app that breaks down allyship into manageable tasks. This app will emulate that by giving users a choice of how they’d like to be active in the Black Lives Matter movement - through donating, educating themselves, e-mailing officials, etc.
  • A post-apocalyptic game where the player is the main character and must work with others to survive. This would use a recommended strategy from our background research that encouraged allies to put others’ needs before their own and to reduce uncertainty in their ability to evaluate and confirm racist incidents.
  • A web/app service where hosts share their hobbies, stories, or skills with guests.The goal is to create shared experiences between people of diverse backgrounds and offer a starting point for relationships to form. Ultimately, by creating moments of connection, our users can find common ground and build empathy.

Pitch and Feedback
After hearing feedback from our classmates, we decided to base our intervention on the accountability app. It would provide users with concrete actions to take, which can build stronger habits and behaviors. The app would also allow the user to tailor tasks to where they are in the process of addressing racism, and how much time/effort they want to devote to the tasks within the app. Out of all of our potential ideas, we felt it had the most potential in terms of stimulating long-term engagement with anti-racial practices.
