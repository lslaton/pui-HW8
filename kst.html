Community on the Go

Increasing audience and host engagement in virtual classes to create more fulfilling experiences through a community-based platform.

Client: Kelly Strayhorn Theater

Context: User-Centered Research and Evaluation Course, CMU

Team: Janie Xue, Piper Hayden, Brent Hong

Role: Design Lead

Timeline: September-December, 2020

Problem
With the COVID-19 pandemic, theaters have been unable to host in-person events and are looking for innovative ways to engage audiences. Kelly Strayhorn Theater, located in the East Liberty neighborhood of Pittsburgh, partnered with our User-Centered Research and Evaluation class to generate experiences to increase audience engagement, donations, and attendance at events while adhering to safety and health guidelines.

"How might we foster more meaningful interactions between participants and hosts that create or increase value in virtual events?"
Solution

Community on the Go
My team worked to create a community-based website, Community on the Go, for virtual events. Participants can directly contact hosts and other attendees, thereby fostering more fulfilling experiences and engagement.

My Role

In addition to working to recruit and interview participants, I worked with my team to generate insights that would create value both for Kelly Strayhorn Theater and its patrons. I served as the Design Lead on our project, and was responsible for generating ideas for solutions that addressed user needs as well as guiding the prototyping process.

Research Process

Client Data Analysis
I began by conducting background research into the theatrical and performance section to see how they have been affected and adapted to the pandemic. My team and I also dove into data provided by KST to learn about previous engagement patterns. We hypothesized that by investing into social media campaigns, there is potential to increase engagement with smaller events.

Background Research
We decided to complete a diverse range of activities so that we could get a comprehensive idea of the scope of theater and performing arts events, including reviewing previous KST events and engaging with theatrical organizations on social media. We found that theaters were having to take less creative risks in programming to maintain their audience while adapting to the virtual format that the pandemic necessitates.

Synthesize, Ideate, & Design
After collecting our team's stakeholder and customer journey maps, storyboards, and prototypes, we began to define our project. Almost every role in the theater will be impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic, but there were now opportunities for audiences to take on a more active role in the theater's programming. We decided to pursue this new problem space to create some sort of platform for patrons to connect with artists, particularly in non-performance events like workshops or classes.

After conducting our initial background research, we established the following research goals:

  • Understand what makes people engage in a virtual events
  • Understand how intuitive virtual streaming platforms are
  • Exploring what class offerings will attract different audiences
User Research

Think-Aloud
To begin our user research, our team had participants think aloud as they found and registered for a virtual event on an experience website. We were interested in learning more about how participants choose which events to attend, including the format and activity that they will be completing. We found that users searched for the value proposition of each event, and selected those events that they felt they could get the most out of.

Contextual Interviews
To learn more about the host's experience with the transition to virtual classes, we interviewed five teachers who utilize various platforms to teach classes, as well as the pain points (and what is going well) for the artists/streamers. How artists define a successful virtual event was also a key insight for us to gauge how streaming platforms may contribute to or inhibit the potential of a virtual class. Our key takeaways are as follows:

  • Technological difficulties and limitations significantly reduce the quality of the event and the host’s ability to effectively host the event.
  • Although hosts are willing to try a variety of strategies to increase participation, audience engagement in virtual events is still a challenge.
  • Pre-event promotion is a large factor in determining audience size and audience engagement during the event.
  • Virtual events create more opportunities for increased accessibility and creativity.
  • Setting clear expectations and goals can help reduce the host’s anxiety when planning and preparing for virtual events, and draw in a larger audience.

Speed Dating
After conducting another Walk the Wall session, our team established four main user needs:

  1. Event atendees want to be able to talk with other classmates or their instructor prior to and after class.
  2. Virtual class participants are uncertain about what to expect when attending a virtual event, such as the activities they’ll be doing in class, what to wear, or if they’ll need any additional equipment or tools.
  3. Attendees want to feel more comfortable interacting with other participants. Otherwise, not only will attendees have less social interactions, but they might miss certain points from the host and be too uncomfortable asking about it, thus feeling lost.
  4. Attendees need to find and select events that suit their interests. In the vast digital space for virtual events, it can be hard to determine exactly which events to attend. Attendees want to ensure that they gain value out of virtual events.
We then created sixteen storyboards of potential solutions to user needs that varied in level of risk. We presented these situations to various users to learn more and uncover their needs, and came away with the following insights:
  1. For recurring events, participants have a strong desire to build long-term rapport with the host and other attendees.
  2. Participants are uncertain about expectations at virtual events, and they rely on the behavior of other attendees to decide what the social norms to follow.
  3. Participants would like to attend events with people they are already comfortable with to chat and ask questions during the event.
